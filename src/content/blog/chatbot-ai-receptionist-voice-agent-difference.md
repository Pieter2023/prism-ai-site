A Kelowna contractor called me Tuesday. He'd been Googling "AI for my business" and landed on three different vendor sites — one selling a chatbot, one selling an AI receptionist, and one selling a voice agent. All three claimed to do the same thing: answer customer questions and book appointments.

He wanted to know which one he actually needed, and whether they were all just different names for the same product.

Short answer: no. They're three fundamentally different tools, built for three different jobs, and picking the wrong one will cost you time and leads.

Here's what each one actually does.

## The three categories, plainly


A chatbot lives on your website and handles text-based questions from browsing visitors. An AI voice receptionist answers your phone and conducts spoken conversations with customers who are ready to book. These are fundamentally different tools built for different moments in the customer journey. Most service businesses get more value from voice AI because their business runs on phone calls, not website chat
.

**Chatbot** — text-only, lives on your website or messaging apps (Facebook Messenger, Instagram DM, WhatsApp). The visitor has to be actively browsing, notice the widget, click it, and type their question.

**AI receptionist** — answers your actual business phone line with a synthetic voice that holds a real conversation, qualifies leads, and books into your calendar. Operates 24/7 on inbound calls.

**Voice agent** — the broader technical term for any AI system that speaks. This includes AI receptionists, outbound sales diallers, appointment reminder bots, and customer-support voice systems. "Voice agent" is the category; "AI receptionist" is one specific use case.

For most Kelowna and BC small businesses, the question boils down to chatbot versus AI receptionist — and the answer depends on where your customers actually are when they want to reach you.

## Where each tool lives (and why that matters)


A chatbot operates on text channels: your website, Facebook Messenger, WhatsApp, Instagram DMs, or any other text-based platform. The customer has to be actively browsing one of these channels, notice the chat widget or message option, choose to engage, and type their query. This is a high-friction path that suits information-seeking behaviour — someone researching options, comparing prices, or looking for answers before they have made a decision
.

Think about the last time you browsed a service business website at 10 PM on a Sunday. You might read the FAQ, check the pricing page, maybe start filling out a contact form. A chatbot works well in that moment — it's low-commitment, lets you ask a quick question without picking up the phone, and gives you an answer without leaving the page.

But that's browsing behaviour, not buying behaviour.


An AI voice receptionist operates on your phone line. It answers when a customer calls your actual business number — the number on your Google Business profile, your business card, your website's contact page. This is a zero-friction path. The customer is already committed enough to pick up the phone and dial. They are not browsing; they are ready to act
.

For most trades, clinics, salons, and service shops in BC, the phone is still where the business happens. Your Google Business listing gets clicked, the caller hits "call", and if nobody picks up, they call the next contractor on the list.


A chatbot cannot book a dental appointment while a patient is driving; an AI voice receptionist can
.

## What chatbots are actually good at

Chatbots handle the early-stage, low-commitment questions that come in when someone is still figuring out whether you're even the right fit.

"What are your hours?"
"Do you service Westbank?"
"How much does X cost?"


The cost difference is compelling, but the real advantage isn't just the money — it's what a chatbot can do that a human receptionist can't. 24/7 availability. Half your inquiries come outside business hours. A chatbot captures every single one. Instant responses
.


AI chatbots handle virtually unlimited simultaneous conversations. There is no hold time, no busy signal, and no "all representatives are currently assisting other customers" message. During peak periods, promotions, or seasonal rushes, this scalability is the difference between capturing demand and losing it
.

But here's the hard truth I tell every client considering a chatbot: 
chatbot interactions introduce a common frustration — the loop. Users type a question, get an answer that does not quite address their specific situation, type a follow-up, and either get a generic response or are told to "call the office." At that point they have wasted time on the chatbot and still need to call. The experience creates friction and mild irritation — not the warm, professional impression you want your business to make
.

A chatbot is a qualified-lead capture tool for the top of the funnel. It's not a closing tool. If your business depends on phone calls to actually book work, a chatbot on its own won't solve the problem.

## What AI receptionists are actually good at


An AI assistant answers calls 24 hours a day, 365 days a year. Evenings, weekends, public holidays, 3 AM on a Tuesday: every call gets answered
.

And unlike voicemail — where 
80% of callers hang up without leaving a message
 — an AI receptionist picks up on the first ring, holds a real conversation, qualifies the lead, and books the appointment into your calendar while you're finishing a job or asleep.


An AI voice receptionist, by contrast, creates a conversation that feels like speaking to a professional team member. Modern voice AI speaks naturally, pauses appropriately, asks the right follow-up questions, and completes the booking without ever needing to redirect the caller elsewhere. For service businesses where the customer relationship starts with this first interaction, the experience difference is significant
.

Here's the part most vendors won't tell you: 
voice AI costs roughly $0.40 per call, compared to $7 to $12 per call for human agents. That's a 90-95% cost reduction per automated interaction
. For a busy Kelowna HVAC shop taking 200 calls a month, that's the difference between $1,400 to $2,400 in human labour and roughly $80 in AI usage fees.

The AI receptionist I build for clients at Prism AI costs **CAD $4,500** as a fixed setup fee, then runs on usage-based telephony and AI API costs that average **$40 to $150 per month** depending on call volume. Compare that to a part-time receptionist at $2,500 to $3,500 per month, and the payback timeline is under two months for most service businesses.

## Where "voice agent" fits in

Voice agent is the umbrella term. It's any AI system that uses synthetic speech to hold a conversation.

An AI receptionist is a voice agent. So is an outbound sales dialler, an appointment-reminder bot, a customer-survey caller, and the voice system your bank uses when you call to check your balance.

The confusion happens because some vendors use "voice agent" to sound more technical, and others use "AI receptionist" to sound more approachable. They're often talking about the same thing.

When you're evaluating vendors, ask this: "Does this answer my business phone line, or does it sit on my website?" That question cuts through the jargon fast.

## Which one your BC business actually needs

Here's the decision tree I walk Kelowna clients through.

**Start with this question: where do your best leads come from?**

If the answer is phone calls — Google Business listing clicks, word-of-mouth referrals, after-hours emergencies, follow-ups from quotes — you need an AI receptionist, not a chatbot.

If the answer is your website, and most of your traffic is people browsing for information before they're ready to commit, a chatbot makes sense as a first step.


The best configuration for most businesses: AI chatbot handles the initial contact, answers routine questions, and captures lead information 24/7. When a conversation becomes complex or emotional, the chatbot routes it to a human team member with full context
.

**Second question: do you currently lose calls because nobody picks up?**

If yes, the AI receptionist is the single highest-ROI automation you can deploy. 
Companies that contact a lead within 5 minutes qualify it 21 times more often. Anyone still sitting on legacy IVR loses the lead to the next vendor that calls back in 10 seconds
.

**Third question: are you drowning in "what are your hours" and "do you do X" questions?**

If yes, and those questions come through your website or social channels, a chatbot clears that noise fast and frees up your time for the work that actually pays.

Most of my Kelowna clients end up with both — chatbot on the website for the browsers, AI receptionist on the phone line for the buyers. The two tools don't compete; they cover different parts of the funnel.

## The local angle: BC PIPA and voice recording

If you're deploying an AI receptionist in BC, you're subject to PIPA — the Personal Information Protection Act. 
Under BC PIPA, organizations must obtain consent before collecting personal information, including voice recordings. The Office of the Information and Privacy Commissioner for BC has issued guidance making clear that audio recordings constitute personal information. Organizations recording calls must identify the purpose for the recording before or at the time of collection, obtain the individual's consent (express or implied, depending on sensitivity), allow individuals to withdraw consent on reasonable notice, and provide alternatives if a person objects to being recorded
.

The good news: 
when you call a bank or telecom company for service a voice may announce that the call will be recorded for quality control and training. By staying on the line and speaking with a service representative, you are implying your consent to the collection of your personal information in the recording for quality control and training. You are not implying your consent for any other purpose and so your personal information in the recording cannot be used or disclosed for any other purpose without consent
.

Implied consent works for most BC service businesses. Your AI receptionist plays a brief disclosure at the start of the call ("This call may be recorded for quality and training purposes"), and if the caller stays on the line, you have consent. Simple, legal, and standard practice across the province.

If you're in a regulated industry — health, legal, finance — talk to someone who knows PIPA before you deploy. The rules tighten when you're handling sensitive personal information.

## One line takeaway

A chatbot answers text questions from website visitors who are browsing. An AI receptionist answers your phone and books the work. Pick the tool that matches where your customers actually are when they want to reach you, and don't let a vendor sell you the wrong one because they only offer one flavour.

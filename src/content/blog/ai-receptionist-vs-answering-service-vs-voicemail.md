A Kelowna dentist asked me last month what the difference is between an AI receptionist and an answering service. She'd been quoted CAD $650 a month by Ruby Receptionists, seen AI receptionist ads for $50, and wondered if voicemail was really that bad.

It's a fair question. The three options sound similar, the pricing is all over the map, and most vendor websites make it worse by promising "never miss a call again" without explaining what's actually happening on the other end of the line.

Here's the breakdown I wish I could have sent her.

## The three ways small businesses handle after-hours and overflow calls in 2026

When you can't pick up the phone—because you're with a client, on a job site, or it's 9 PM—your call goes one of three places. Each handles it differently. Each costs differently. And each suits a different kind of business.

### Option 1: Voicemail

**What it is:** The caller hears a greeting, leaves a message (maybe), and you return the call later (maybe).

**What it costs:** Free, or bundled into your phone plan.

**What it actually does:**
Voicemail is passive. 
85% of callers who don't reach someone won't call back
—they move to the next listing on Google. If the call came in at 6 PM on a Friday and you don't check messages until Monday morning, the lead is three days cold. For a plumber with an emergency call, a realtor during spring market, or a clinic fielding new-patient inquiries, that's revenue walking out the door.

**Who it suits:** Businesses where calls are low-priority or where customers will wait. Internal company lines. Personal mobile numbers.

**Who it doesn't suit:** Any business where missing a call costs you money.

### Option 2: Live answering service (human agents)

**What it is:** Real people—usually in North America—answer your calls, take messages, qualify leads, book appointments, and transfer urgent calls. Services like Ruby Receptionists, AnswerConnect, and Smith.ai have been doing this for years.

**What it costs in Canada:**

Ruby Receptionist charges approximately $245 for 50 minutes per month
, and that's the entry tier. Most small businesses with moderate call volume land in the CAD $300 to $650 per month range. Some services charge per call (CAD $0.75 to $1.50 per call); others charge per minute with overages that add up fast.

**What it actually does:**
A live agent picks up in your business name, follows a script you provide, captures caller details, and routes the call or books it into your calendar. The agents are trained, friendly, and available 24/7 on most plans. The experience feels human because it is.

The downside is cost. 
One reviewer reported being charged 200 minutes for $650 per month
 with Ruby, and competitors told them the same tier elsewhere was half the price. The other downside is that the agents don't know your business the way an in-house person would. They work from a script. If a caller asks something off-script, the agent takes a message and you follow up.

**Who it suits:** Professional services (law, accounting, real estate) where the caller expects a polished, human interaction and where the lifetime value of one client justifies the monthly cost. Businesses that need bilingual coverage (English and French) or highly customised call handling.

**Who it doesn't suit:** High-volume service businesses (trades, clinics, salons) where CAD $500+ per month is a tough ROI when most calls are "What time are you open?" or "Can I book for Thursday?"

### Option 3: AI voice receptionist

**What it is:** A voice AI model answers your phone, holds a real conversation in natural language, qualifies the caller, books into your calendar, and texts you a summary. It runs 24/7 with no per-call ceiling.

**What it costs in Canada:**
Setup ranges from CAD $4,500 for a custom build (what I charge at Prism AI) to monthly SaaS subscriptions. 
Canadian AI answering services like Ask Benny charge approximately $15–25 per month for low call volumes (30–40 calls)
, though most scale to $50–$300 per month depending on features and volume. The key difference from live services: 
AI voice agents typically cost between $50 and $300 per month
, a fraction of human-staffed alternatives.

**What it actually does:**

AI voice agents use natural language processing to understand, respond to, and take action on phone calls just like a human receptionist would, creating natural, human-like interactions
 rather than the robotic phone trees of the past. The AI answers instantly, asks qualifying questions ("What service are you calling about?", "What's a good callback number?"), 
accesses your calendar in real-time, finds available slots, books appointments, and sends automated confirmations and reminders—reducing no-shows by up to 25%
 in some industries.

The voice quality in 2026 is shockingly good. Callers often don't realise they're speaking to AI until you tell them. The system integrates with your CRM, your calendar (Google, Outlook, Cal.com), and your booking flow.

The honest limitation: AI handles structured conversations well—booking, FAQs, lead capture. It struggles with edge cases, highly emotional calls, or anything that requires empathy and judgement. For those, you need a human backup or an escalation path.

**Who it suits:** Service businesses with predictable inquiry types (trades, clinics, salons, real estate, field services) where speed matters more than polish and where $4,500 upfront or $100–$300 per month delivers better ROI than $500+ for live agents. Businesses that get after-hours calls or overflow during busy periods. Anyone who's currently losing leads to voicemail.

**Who it doesn't suit:** Businesses where every call is high-stakes and requires nuanced judgement (complex legal intake, financial advisory, crisis intervention). Industries with compliance requirements that prohibit automated call handling without human oversight.

## The decision framework I use with clients

When a Kelowna business owner asks me which option to pick, I walk them through three questions:

**1. What's the cost of a missed call?**
If one missed lead is worth CAD $2,000 to you (a realtor, a roofer, a cosmetic dentist), voicemail is a leak you can't afford. If the average call is someone asking for your hours, voicemail might be fine.

**2. How many calls do you get, and when?**
If you're fielding 10 calls a month, a live answering service at CAD $245 is overkill. If you're getting 80 calls a month and half come after 5 PM, AI makes sense. If you're getting 200+ calls and they're all high-value, a live service might justify the cost.

**3. What does the caller actually need?**
If the caller needs empathy, reassurance, or complex problem-solving, lean human. If the caller needs an appointment time, a price quote, or routing to the right person, AI handles it.

## What I tell the "I'm not sure yet" crowd

Start with the lowest-friction option that solves the immediate problem. If you're losing leads to voicemail right now, an AI receptionist at CAD $100–$300 per month is a no-brainer. If you try it for 90 days and find you need a human touch, upgrade to a live service. If you're a solo operation just testing the water, try one of the low-cost AI options (Ask Benny, Dialbox) on a per-minute model and see what your actual usage looks like.

The worst move is doing nothing because you're overwhelmed by options.

Most small businesses I work with in Kelowna land on AI. The math works: 
a full-time receptionist earning the median wage costs approximately $44,000 to $60,000 per year when you factor in benefits, payroll taxes, training, and turnover, translating to $3,700 to $5,000 monthly, and you're still limited to business hours
. A live answering service cuts that to CAD $300–$650 but still charges per minute. An AI receptionist runs CAD $100–$300 per month, answers every call, and scales without adding cost.

The best call-handling system is the one that actually gets implemented this quarter—not the one you're still researching in six months.

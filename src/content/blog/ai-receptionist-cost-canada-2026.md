## What "AI receptionist" actually means in 2026

The phrase covers two different things and the price gap between them is enormous.

The first is a **chat-style AI receptionist on your website**. This is a bot that captures form submissions, books appointments, and asks qualifying questions. These usually run USD $30 to $150 a month per site if you buy a SaaS product, or one-time CAD $500 to $2,500 if you have one built and host it yourself.

The second is a **voice-based AI receptionist that answers your phone**. You get a dedicated phone number where a synthetic voice picks up, holds a real conversation, captures the lead, and books into your calendar. This is the one that's been getting the press in 2026, and it's the one most small businesses mean when they say "AI receptionist."

Everything below is about the voice version. That's where the real money is, both saved and spent.

## The three real buying paths

When a small business owner picks up the phone (so to speak), they've got three options. Here's the honest comparison.

### Option 1: Hire a human receptionist

Median pay for a receptionist in British Columbia in 2025 to 2026 sits in the **CAD $22 to $28 per hour** range, depending on industry and location, per public wage data on [Job Bank Canada](https://www.jobbank.gc.ca/). Loaded for CPP, EI, vacation accrual, and benefits, you're looking at **CAD $55,000 to $72,000 per year** for one full-time seat.

That's one person working roughly 40 hours a week. Calls outside those hours go to voicemail.

### Option 2: Outsource to a virtual receptionist service

Services like Ruby Receptionists, Smith.ai, and AnswerConnect bundle live agents on a per-minute or per-call model. Plans typically start around **USD $300 per month** for low-volume use (~30 to 50 calls), scaling fast as volume grows. For a busy local trade or clinic, **USD $500 to $1,200 a month is normal**.

These services answer 24/7 and the agents are real humans. The downside is that the agents don't know your business, can't access your calendar without integration setup, and switch off the moment your monthly minutes are used up.

### Option 3: Build a custom AI voice receptionist

This is what I do at Prism AI, and what most "AI receptionist 2026" content is referring to.

You get a dedicated phone number that answers in milliseconds, holds a real conversation in your brand voice, books into your calendar, qualifies the lead, and texts you afterwards with a structured summary. It runs 24/7 with no per-call ceiling.

**At Prism AI, the setup fee for a custom AI receptionist is CAD $4,500**, and the build timeline is two to three weeks. That includes the voice persona, phone number provisioning, calendar integration, lead-capture flow, and the after-call notification system.

What it doesn't include, and this is where some vendors get cute, is **ongoing usage cost**. The underlying voice synthesis (typically a provider like [ElevenLabs](https://elevenlabs.io/pricing)) charges per minute of conversation, and the language model behind the conversation has its own per-token cost. For a typical Kelowna small business, the all-in monthly running cost lands somewhere between CAD $40 and CAD $200 depending on call volume. We scope this together up front so you know exactly what you're committing to.

## The hidden cost everyone forgets: missed calls

Every receptionist comparison should start with this question, but almost none do.

**What is one missed call worth to you?**

For a roofing contractor whose average inspection-to-job conversion is around 30% and whose average job is CAD $4,500, one missed call that should have become an inspection is a **CAD $1,350 expected-value loss**. Two of those a month wipes out the entire receptionist budget for any of the three options above.

Industry research over the past several years has consistently put SMB missed-call rates at 20% to 30% of inbound volume during business hours, and far higher after-hours. I won't quote a specific stat with false precision because the numbers vary by source and methodology, but the directional truth is well-established. Most small businesses miss more calls than their owners think, and most of those callers don't leave a voicemail. They go to the next listing on Google.

This is the part that makes the AI receptionist math work. It's not really competing with a human receptionist on cost. It's competing with a voicemail box, and voicemail loses.

## When an AI receptionist is the wrong answer

Honesty matters more than the sale here.

If your business depends on emotional hand-holding for distressed callers (bereavement services, certain medical practices, crisis support), an AI voice agent is the wrong call. Not because it can't hold the conversation. It can. But because your callers will resent it, and that resentment costs you trust.

If your call volume is genuinely low (fewer than five inbound calls a week), the math doesn't yet work. Use Google Voice, set up a clean voicemail with a callback promise, and revisit when you're growing.

If you already employ a great receptionist who is the warm voice of your business, an AI agent should be **after-hours overflow**, not a replacement. That changes the math but not the architecture.

## The takeaway

In Canada in 2026, the price spread for receptionist coverage looks like this. Voicemail is free, but expensive in lost leads. Virtual receptionist services run roughly USD $300 to $1,200 a month with capped capacity. A human full-time hire costs CAD $55,000 or more per year, working 40 hours a week. A custom AI receptionist runs CAD $4,500 setup plus modest ongoing usage, working 24/7.

The right answer depends entirely on call volume, your average lead value, and whether you've actually measured what you're missing. If you haven't measured, that's the first thing to do, not buy anything.
